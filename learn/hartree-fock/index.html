Hartree–Fock calculation in your browser

The laboratory solves a real self-consistent-field problem locally. This page explains what that statement means, what RHF and UHF can reveal, and where their answers stop being quantitative.

Run H₂O with STO-3G

The approximation

Hartree–Fock represents an N-electron state by one Slater determinant. Each electron moves in the average Coulomb and exchange field generated by the occupied orbitals. The determinant enforces fermionic antisymmetry exactly, so exchange and the Pauli principle are present. Instantaneous correlation between opposite-spin electrons is not.

The energy is varied with respect to the orbital coefficients. Because the effective Fock operator depends on the orbitals it is solving for, the equations are nonlinear and must be iterated to self-consistency.

What the SCF loop does

  1. Build an initial density matrix from a molecular-orbital guess.
  2. Construct the Fock matrix from one- and two-electron integrals.
  3. Solve the generalized eigenvalue problem in a non-orthogonal Gaussian basis.
  4. Update the density, mix or extrapolate it with DIIS, and repeat until the energy and density stabilize.

The app records convergence, iteration count, basis, spin treatment and a reproducibility key. A converged SCF solution is necessary, but it does not prove that the model or basis is accurate enough for a particular scientific claim.

RHF versus UHF

Restricted Hartree–Fock (RHF) uses the same spatial orbital for paired α and β electrons. It is the natural choice for ordinary closed-shell molecules such as H₂O or CH₄. Unrestricted Hartree–Fock (UHF) permits separate α and β orbitals and is used for O₂, NO, OH and other open-shell systems.

UHF can describe spin polarization and a sensible dissociation limit, but its determinant may not be an eigenfunction of S². The displayed ⟨S²⟩ value therefore matters: a large departure from the exact spin value signals spin contamination.

Why the basis changes the answer

The wavefunction is expanded in atom-centred Gaussian functions. STO-3G is minimal and fast; 6-31G splits the valence space; 6-31G* adds polarization functions. A larger basis usually lowers the variational HF energy, but basis improvement does not add the missing electron correlation.

Interpretation rule.

Use STO-3G for fast qualitative pictures. Compare bases before trusting a trend. Treat dipoles, orbital gaps, Koopmans ionization potentials and harmonic frequencies as model-dependent estimates.

What is computed and what is not

The engine computes RHF/UHF energies, orbitals, density-derived maps, Mulliken charges, Mayer bond orders, dipoles, numerical geometry optimization and harmonic vibrations. For two-electron systems it also solves full configuration interaction in the selected small basis.

It does not implement DFT, MP2, coupled cluster, relativistic corrections or radiative QED corrections. The cavity and scaling panels are explicitly pedagogical toy models. For publication work, export the geometry or template and validate it with a production quantum-chemistry package.

Break the H₂ bond: RHF, UHF and full CI

Hydrogen is the smallest molecule, yet stretching its bond exposes the central limitation of a single-determinant wavefunction. The app lets you follow the energy and density while the nuclei separate.

Open the H₂ experiment

Start near equilibrium

At the experimental distance Rₑ ≈ 0.741 Å, both electrons predominantly occupy the bonding σg orbital. RHF is a useful approximation here: its single determinant captures the covalent bond qualitatively, and the density is concentrated between the nuclei.

The app overlays three different objects. The yellow curve is computed Hartree–Fock energy. The blue curve is a Morse potential constructed from experimental spectroscopic constants. The green curve is full configuration interaction for the two electrons, exact only within the chosen finite basis.

Why restricted Hartree–Fock fails

RHF requires one doubly occupied spatial orbital at every distance. At large R that orbital remains a symmetric combination of the two atomic 1s functions. The determinant therefore contains an unphysical mixture of neutral and ionic fragments rather than exactly one electron on each atom.

The error is static correlation: two determinants become equally important, but RHF permits only one. On the plot the RHF curve approaches the wrong dissociation limit. A larger basis improves flexibility but does not remove this one-determinant failure.

What UHF repairs—and breaks

UHF allows the α orbital to localize on one atom and the β orbital on the other. The energy then reaches a physically sensible separated-atom limit. However, the resulting determinant is no longer a pure singlet and mixes in triplet character. This is visible as spin contamination in ⟨S²⟩.

Symmetry breaking is therefore useful but not exact: UHF trades spin purity for a qualitatively correct dissociation picture.

How full CI restores correlation

For two electrons the application can include every determinant available in the selected orbital basis. Full CI mixes the bonding and antibonding configurations with R-dependent coefficients. Near equilibrium the bonding determinant dominates; at large R the combination removes the ionic terms and gives two neutral hydrogen atoms.

Exact has a qualifier.

Full CI is exact for the chosen finite Gaussian basis, not for the complete physical Hilbert space. STO-3G full CI still has basis-set error.

Reproduce the comparison

  1. Open H₂ with STO-3G and wait for the E(R) scan.
  2. Drag R from equilibrium toward 3–5 Å and watch the density split.
  3. Compare the yellow RHF and green FCI tails.
  4. Repeat with 6-31G and distinguish basis error from correlation error.
  5. Export JSON or the report bundle to record method, basis and request key.

Why this matters beyond H₂

The same multireference problem appears in bond breaking, diradicals, transition-metal chemistry and excited states. H₂ is valuable because the failure is visible without chemical complexity. It teaches when a plausible orbital picture is not enough.
